Combinations & Permutations

Combinations & Permutations

Counting principles, factorial notation, permutations, combinations, and arrangement problems.

Created: December 15, 2025

Overview

Basic combinatorics studies how to count arrangements and selections. This page introduces factorial notation, permutations (ordered arrangements), combinations (unordered selections), and simple examples to decide which formula applies.

Factorial

The factorial of a non-negative integer n is
n! = n × (n-1) × (n-2) × … × 1, with 0! = 1.

Permutations (order matters)

Number of ordered arrangements of k items chosen from n distinct items: P(n, k) = n! / (n - k)!.

Example: How many 3-letter arrangements can be made from the letters A, B, C, D? P(4, 3) = 4! / (4-3)! = 4 × 3 × 2 = 24.

Combinations (order does not matter)

Number of unordered selections of k items from n distinct items: C(n, k) = n! / (k! (n - k)!).

Example: How many ways to choose 2 people from 4? C(4, 2) = 4! / (2! 2!) = 6 (AB, AC, AD, BC, BD, CD).

Quick rule

If order matters use permutations; if order doesn't matter use combinations. You can convert between them: P(n,k) = C(n,k) × k!.
